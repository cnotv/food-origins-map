# Food Origins Map — User Guide

Food Origins Map is an interactive world map that shows **where the foods we eat
were first domesticated**. Each pin sits at the historically-cited birthplace of
a crop — the Andes for the potato, the Fertile Crescent for wheat, New Guinea
for the banana — and opens a short origin story with nutrition facts.

This guide covers everything you can do as a visitor. No account or setup is
needed; it runs entirely in your browser.

---

## The map at a glance

When the app loads you see a clean, label-free world map covered in small round
**image badges**. Each badge is one food, pinned at its place of origin and
color-outlined by category:

| Outline color | Category |
|---------------|----------|
| 🔴 Red‑orange | Fruit |
| 🟢 Green | Vegetable |
| 🟡 Gold | Legume |
| 🟣 Purple | Herb & spice |

The map deliberately hides roads, borders, and place names so nothing competes
with the food markers. The faint land and sea shapes are just there for
orientation.

> If a food's photo hasn't loaded, its badge falls back to a colored circle with
> the food's first letter. The category color is preserved either way.

## Finding a food

There are three ways to home in on what you're interested in.

### 1. Pan and zoom

- **Drag** anywhere to pan across the world.
- **Scroll / pinch**, or use the **+ / −** buttons in the top-left corner, to
  zoom in and out.
- The map wraps around, so you can keep dragging east or west without hitting an
  edge.

### 2. Let crowded pins spread out

With hundreds of foods on the map, some regions — the Mediterranean, South and
Southeast Asia, Mesoamerica — are packed with origins. To keep badges readable:

- Where there **isn't room** to show every badge without them overlapping, they
  **group into a single numbered cluster** (a circle showing how many foods are
  hidden inside).
- **Zoom in** or **click a cluster** and it **splits apart**, animating the
  individual food badges outward into their true positions as space opens up.
- Where there **is** enough room, foods always show as their own image — they
  only group up when they would otherwise collide.

This means the map is never cluttered, and the level of detail follows how far
you've zoomed in.

### 3. Filter by category

The bar across the top has filter chips: **All**, **Fruit**, **Vegetable**,
**Legume**, **Herb & spice**. Tap one to show only that category; the map
redraws instantly with just those foods. Tap **All** to bring everything back.

## Reading a food's story

**Click any food badge** to open its detail panel. The panel slides in from the
side and shows:

- **A hero photo** of the food.
- **Its name and category.**
- **Origin region** — the area where it was first domesticated.
- **The origin story** — a short, sourced paragraph on where the food came from
  and how it spread around the world.
- **A nutrition table** — calories, carbohydrates, fiber, and protein per 100 g,
  plus a few notable nutritional highlights (for example "Rich in lycopene" for
  tomatoes).
- **A TasteAtlas link** to read more about the food.

When you open a food, the map also gently recenters on its pin so you can see
exactly where in the world it belongs.

### Closing the panel

- Click the **✕** in the panel, **or**
- Press the **Esc** key, **or**
- Select a different food to swap the panel's contents.

## Tips

- **Compare a whole category's spread.** Filter to *Legume* and zoom out to see
  how beans, lentils, and peas were domesticated in clusters across the Americas,
  the Fertile Crescent, Africa, and Asia.
- **Explore a region.** Zoom into one area and let the clusters split to browse
  every food that originated there.
- **Follow the outlines.** Scanning by color is a quick way to spot, say, all the
  herbs and spices at a glance.

## Frequently asked

**Why are some pins stacked in the same spot?**
Many crops were domesticated in the same cradle (the Fertile Crescent gave us
wheat, barley, lentils, chickpeas, and peas). Their pins sit close together and
group into a cluster until you zoom in.

**Are the locations exact?**
They're pinned at the *historically-cited center of domestication* for each crop
— a scholarly best estimate, not a precise coordinate. The origin story explains
the reasoning for each.

**Why did a badge turn into a letter?**
The food's photo couldn't be loaded, so the app shows a colored initial instead.
The information in the side panel is unaffected.

**Does it work on my phone?**
Yes. On narrow screens the top bar stacks vertically and all touch gestures
(drag, pinch-zoom, tap) work as expected.
